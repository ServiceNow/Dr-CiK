OCEANOGRAPHIC RESEARCH INSTITUTE
TECHNICAL BRIEF: COASTAL UPWELLING DYNAMICS
Report Ref: ORI-PHYS-TBL-07

1.0 PHYSICAL MECHANICS AND FORCING

The initiation of coastal upwelling is primarily driven by the interaction between wind-driven surface stress and the rotation of the Earth. According to observations recorded at monitoring installations such as the Station Alpha-7 Deep-Sea Assembly, wind stress acting parallel to the coastline facilitates a Coriolis-driven mass movement of upper ocean layers. This mechanical forcing results in the transport of surface water away from the shoreline.

2.0 HORIZONTAL DIVERGENCE

As the upper layer progresses in an offshore trajectory, the net displacement of surface water creates a distinct horizontal divergence. This divergence depletes nearshore mass, as the volume of water moving away from the coastal boundary exceeds the volume of water being moved toward it by wind-driven currents. This creates a localized imbalance in the upper water column characterized by a significant reduction in surface-layer density and mass.

3.0 VERTICAL REPLACEMENT DYNAMICS

To satisfy the principle of mass conservation, the localized deficit of surface volume necessitates a vertical replacement from deeper, more compact layers. The void created by horizontal divergence is filled by a compensatory upward flow (upwelling). This process draws water from depths below the mixed layer to the surface. Data regarding vertical current velocity components (WCUR) provide the basis for calculating the rate at which this subsurface volume ascends to restore the horizontal mass equilibrium.

4.0 SCOPE LIMITATIONS

This technical brief is restricted to the physical mechanics of water displacement and volume replacement. The scope of this document excludes the analysis of biological impacts, temperature changes, nutrient cycles, or specific geographic case studies. The objective of this report is to define the hydrodynamic framework under which vertical mass movement is sustained within coastal zones.

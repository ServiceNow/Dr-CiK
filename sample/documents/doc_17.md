Marine Instrumentation & Data Quality Department
Technical Report: Technical Assessment of ADCP Sensor Drift Post-Seismic Activity

1. Executive Summary
This technical assessment details the analysis of time-series observations from Acoustic Doppler Current Profiler (ADCP) hosted sensor assemblies following a documented seismic event. The investigation focuses on the interaction between sea water temperature and pressure (PRES) sensors to determine the validity of recent data anomalies. Findings indicate that observed temperature fluctuations are not representative of thermal shifts in the water column but are artifacts of vertical instrument displacement.

2. Interaction Analysis: Temperature and Pressure (PRES)
Following the seismic event, data streams from the ADCP-hosted assembly showed a high correlation between sea water temperature shifts and variations in the Pressure (PRES) sensor readings. Under normal operating conditions, these sensors operate independently within a stabilized depth stratum. However, post-event data indicates a simultaneous shift in both parameters. 

Detailed analysis of the time-series observations reveals that the temperature sensor is recording values consistent with deeper, colder water masses, which corresponds precisely with an increase in pressure readings. This confirms a direct mechanical interaction where the pressure sensor's data reflects a new vertical position, subsequently influencing the ambient temperature being recorded by the assembly.

3. Root Cause Identification: Seafloor Subsidence
Based on the trajectory of the instrument displacement, the department has identified seafloor subsidence triggered by the seismic event as the root cause of the shift. The instrument frame, originally leveled on the seabed, moved downward into a new bathymetric position. This seismic event-driven displacement resulted in a sudden change in the instrument’s vertical coordinate relative to the surface.

4. Data Masking and Artifact Documentation
A primary concern addressed in this report is how changes in instrument depth and pressure mask actual seawater temperature trends. Because seawater is thermally stratified, the downward vertical displacement of the sensor assembly into higher-pressure zones causes it to enter cooler thermal layers. 

There is clear evidence that the observed temperature fluctuations are an artifact of vertical displacement rather than actual thermal shifts in the environment. The sensor is functioning correctly; however, it is now sampling a different portion of the water column. This mechanical drift creates a false appearance of rapid cooling or thermal instability that does not reflect the actual state of the sea water at the original monitoring depth.

5. Forecast and Stabilization
The Marine Instrumentation & Data Quality Department forecasts that the apparent temperature drift will cease once pressure readings—and by extension, the instrument depth—stabilize. Once the ADCP assembly reaches a state of equilibrium on the post-seismic seafloor, the metadata for the pressure sensor can be recalibrated. As soon as the vertical movement is arrested and PRES readings become constant, the temperature sensor will again provide a reliable time-series of the ambient thermal conditions at that new fixed depth.

6. Scope Limitations
This assessment is strictly limited to the mechanical and sensor-based interaction between depth / pressure and temperature readings. It does not address broader ecological impacts, tectonic regional mapping, or specific equipment repair logs.

TITLE: SITE SURVEY AND INFRASTRUCTURE GUIDE FOR THE ARID HEIGHTS RESEARCH REGION

1.0 HISTORICAL NOMENCLATURE: ARID HEIGHTS RESEARCH ANNEX
The facility currently designated as the Arid Heights Research Annex has undergone several naming iterations since its inception. Originally established as the 'High Desert Observation Post,' the site was formally absorbed into the regional network as 'Research Station 7.' Following the 1988 administrative restructuring, it was briefly titled the 'Vertical Aridity Lab' before the Regional Meteorological and Geological Survey Office settled on the current designation: Arid Heights Research Annex. All historical records filed under these legacy titles refer to the same geographic footprint.

2.0 GEOLOGICAL COMPOSITION: THE HIGH PLAINS MERIDIAN
The High Plains Meridian serves as the primary longitudinal datum for the region. Geological core sampling and surface analysis at this specific site reveal a complex substrate composition. The primary matrix consists of highly compacted ferruginous sandstone, overlaid by a 1.2-meter layer of desiccated aeolian silt. At deeper strata (ranging from 5 to 12 meters), the composition transitions into a dense metamorphic bedrock with significant quartz veining. This structural stability is the primary reason for the placement of high-mass geological instruments at the Meridian.

3.0 SOLAR HORIZONTAL SURFACE DEFINITIONS
For the purposes of localized site measurements and the calibration of irradiance sensors, the following horizontal surface definitions are utilized at the Arid Heights Research Region:

- Primary Reference Plane (PRP): The theoretical flat surface tangent to the Earth's curvature at the High Plains Meridian.
- Effective Absorptive Surface (EAS): The physical plane of the photovoltaic arrays, adjusted for fixed-mount inclination.
- Local Ground Horizontal (LGH): The mean grade of the natural terrain surrounding the Annex, used for measuring reflected shortwave radiation (albedo).
- Clear-Sky Datum: A virtual horizontal surface used to calculate the maximum theoretical solar influx at the research site's specific altitude.
